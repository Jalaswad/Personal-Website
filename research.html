The history of visible life
Phanerozoic · 541 Ma → Present

What drives global taxonomic homogenization in deep time?

Examining the history of marine invertebrate life to determine the contributions of climate change and extinction to community compositional similarity, building a statistical framework that separates climate signal from noise across hundreds of millions of years. Now in review at Proceedings of the Royal Society B.

Graphical abstract, drawn by Jood

A graphical abstract I drew for Payne et al. 2023. The core question: as Earth warms, do marine communities worldwide begin to look alike? The answer from deep time is yes, and understanding why matters for predicting what climate change will do to today's oceans.

The end-Permian Mass Extinction
~252 million years ago

The Great Dulling: homogenization after Earth's worst mass extinction

After the largest mass extinction in Earth's history, marine ecosystems became strikingly similar worldwide, what we called "The Great Dulling" in Stanford Magazine. Organisms that could tolerate warmer, lower-oxygen oceans expanded globally, erasing the rich regional distinctiveness that had characterized pre-extinction communities.

I also spent a field season in Saudi Arabia collecting and systematically identifying bivalve fossils from directly after the extinction event, building one of the first primary datasets of this kind from the Arabian Peninsula.

Read in Science Advances Discover Magazine
Al Aswad et al. 2025, Science Advances

Published in Science Advances, 2025. The paper demonstrates that end-Permian warming drove survivors with broad physiological tolerances to expand globally, producing the homogenized "Great Dulling" signature we see in the fossil record.

Conservation Paleobiology
Late Pleistocene · ~125,000 years ago

Humans or nature? Disentangling drivers of modern range shifts

Current projects shift to conservation paleobiology, using the MIS 5e (Last Interglacial) fossil record as a pre-human baseline to separate climate-driven from human-mediated range expansions in modern marine communities along the California coast.

A central finding has emerged: no Northern Panamic bivalve species has established north of Point Conception in the modern record, despite model predictions from the fossil record. All documented modern arrivals are Atlantic, Asian, or Mediterranean species, suggesting that the pathways for natural climate-crossing have been fundamentally disrupted.

Future projections
Now → 2100

Baselines for predicting what comes next

By examining data at multiple temporal and spatial scales, I aim to understand how the traits that helped organisms survive past environmental change will help, or fail to help, them survive future ones.

The metabolic index, which quantifies the ratio of oxygen supply to demand for ectotherms, offers a physiologically grounded framework for projecting future habitat loss. My work bridges deep-time calibration with Earth system model output to ground-truth those projections in the fossil record.
